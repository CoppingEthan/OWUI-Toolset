## When to use

The user wants help writing an email. They might give you the goal and audience, or they might just paste the situation and expect you to figure it out. Either way, the goal is an email the recipient will actually read and act on.

## Before drafting — clarify

1. **Recipient** — who, and what's the existing relationship? (cold, warm, internal, exec, customer, candidate, vendor)
2. **Outcome you want** — what specific action should they take after reading? Reply with X? Approve Y? Forward to Z? Block 30 minutes?
3. **Context they need** — what background must they have to act on it? Cut anything else.
4. **Tone** — formal, casual, friendly, urgent, apologetic, firm. Match the relationship and the asks.
5. **Constraints** — length, deadlines, attachments, whether you can be funny/personal.

If the user gave you a thread to reply to, read what's already been said and react to it specifically. Don't restate what the other person already wrote.

## Universal principles

- **Subject lines win or lose the email.** Specific > clever. "Quick question on Q3 hiring plan" >> "Touching base".
- **First sentence states the ask or the point.** Reader should know within 5 seconds why you wrote.
- **One ask per email.** If there are two, the second one gets ignored.
- **Short paragraphs, short sentences.** Most email is read on phones in elevators.
- **Make the action obvious.** End with a clear next step ("Can you confirm by Friday EOD?", not "Let me know your thoughts").
- **No filler openings.** Skip "I hope this email finds you well" and "I just wanted to follow up". Get to the point.
- **Cut "just".** It signals timidity. "Just checking in" → "Checking in" (or better: a direct ask).
- **Read it back.** If you wouldn't have time to read it as the recipient, trim until you would.

## Format templates by type

### Cold outreach

```
Subject: [specific reason you're writing — not "introduction"]

Hi [name],

[1 sentence: who you are + why you're reaching out to them specifically — show you know who they are]

[1-2 sentences: what's interesting / relevant — make this about them, not you]

[1 sentence: the ask — small, easy to say yes to]

[Sign-off]
```

Total: 4–6 sentences. If it's longer, you haven't got to the point.

### Internal ask

```
Subject: [specific ask + by when, e.g. "Need API key by Thursday"]

Hi [name],

[1 sentence: what you need + by when]

[1 sentence: why / context]

[Optional: links to relevant docs]

Thanks,
[name]
```

### Follow-up after no reply

```
Subject: [reply-prefix the original, or "Following up: [original subject]"]

Hi [name],

[1 sentence: bumping this — assume good faith]

[1 sentence: re-state the ask + deadline]

[1 line offering to make it easier — "happy to jump on a quick call" / "let me know if you'd rather I redirect this"]

Thanks,
[name]
```

Don't apologise for following up. Don't say "I know you're busy". Just bump.

### Status update / heads up

```
Subject: [update on X / FYI: Y / Status — Z]

Hi all,

[1-2 sentences: the update]

[Bullets if there are multiple points:]
- Point 1
- Point 2

[1 sentence: what (if anything) you need from them, or "no action needed"]

[Sign-off]
```

### Reply to a difficult email

- Acknowledge what they said before responding to it.
- Don't match heat with heat. Match heat with calm + specifics.
- Stick to facts and concrete next steps.
- If emotional, draft, leave it for an hour, re-read, then send.

### Apology / fix-it email

```
Subject: [direct, e.g. "Re: missed deadline on X — recovery plan"]

Hi [name],

[1 sentence: own the issue — no excuses, no "circumstances", no "challenges"]

[1-2 sentences: what's already been done + what's next, with a deadline]

[1 sentence: how you'll prevent it next time, briefly]

[Sign-off]
```

### Hiring / candidate email

- For "yes, move forward": warm, specific to their conversation, clear next step + timeline.
- For "no, not moving forward": prompt, brief, kind, doesn't oversell. "We've decided to move forward with another candidate. Wishing you the best."
- For interview scheduling: propose 2–3 specific times, not "let me know your availability".

### Sales / commercial

- Lead with the customer's situation, not your product.
- Concrete number or proof point > marketing language.
- Ask for the smallest next step that moves it forward (15-min call, not "demo + contract review").

## Avoid

- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "Just wanted to circle back"
- "Per my last email"
- "Please advise"
- "Touching base"
- "Hope you had a great weekend!"
- "As discussed" (unless you literally just discussed it)
- "Thoughts?" as a closing — be specific about the ask
- ALL CAPS WORDS, multiple exclamation marks, ¶ excessive ¶ formatting
- Long preambles before the actual point

## Output

Default: deliver one polished version, with the subject line on its own line at the top. If the user asked for variants, give 2 with a brief label for each (e.g. "warmer tone" / "more direct").

If you needed clarifying info you didn't get, draft with placeholders in square brackets like `[insert deadline]` so the user can fill them in.
